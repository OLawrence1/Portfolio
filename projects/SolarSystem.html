Overview

A custom 3D solar system simulation developed for my Maths for Games module. The project was designed to demonstrate the practical application of core game mathematics, including vectors, matrices, Euler angles, quaternions, and interpolation.

The simulation allows the user to navigate around a miniature solar system while planets orbit, rotate, and scale correctly using mathematically driven transformations rather than pre-built Unity functionality.

My Contribution

This was an individual project where I was responsible for the entire implementation, including the mathematical systems, camera controls, orbital movement, transformation calculations, and evaluation of different rotation and interpolation techniques.

A major focus of the project was applying mathematical concepts taught throughout the module to solve practical gameplay and simulation problems.

Technical Breakdown

The simulation uses vector mathematics extensively for positioning and directional calculations. Planetary orbit movement, camera navigation, and object transformations all rely on vector operations to calculate movement within 3D space.

For object transformations, matrix mathematics was used to combine translation, rotation, and scaling operations. This required constructing transformations in the correct order to avoid unexpected results and ensure objects maintained their intended positions and orientations.

Both Euler angles and quaternions were explored for handling rotation. Euler angles provided a simple method for controlling camera pitch and yaw, while quaternions were used for planetary rotation to avoid gimbal lock and ensure stable orientation regardless of rotational state.

Interpolation techniques were implemented to smoothly transition the camera between planets. Linear interpolation was used to avoid abrupt camera snapping and create a more natural navigation experience when moving throughout the solar system.

Challenges

One of the main technical challenges encountered was gimbal lock when using Euler angles for object rotation. This caused rotational axes to become aligned, producing unpredictable behaviour. The issue was resolved by replacing object rotations with quaternions, providing stable orientation regardless of rotational state.

Another challenge involved matrix transformation order. Early implementations produced incorrect results because transformations were being applied in the wrong sequence. Through testing and debugging, the correct Scale → Rotate → Translate pipeline was implemented.

Camera interpolation also introduced issues at high movement speeds, where the camera struggled to fully reach its target position. This was addressed by adjusting interpolation rates to balance smooth movement and responsiveness.

Outcome

The final simulation successfully demonstrated the practical application of multiple mathematical concepts commonly used in game development. It provided hands-on experience implementing vectors, matrices, quaternions, and interpolation while also highlighting common challenges such as gimbal lock and transformation ordering.

The project significantly improved my understanding of the mathematics that underpin 3D game engines and provided a strong foundation for later gameplay and AI programming projects.
